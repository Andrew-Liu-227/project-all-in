What Can Economics Teach Us?
Doug Coate • Economics Professor • Rutgers, Newark
“History provides strong evidence for capitalism or the market price system as the preferred system of social organization. Without it, as the past (and the present) demonstrate, people struggle to survive.”
  • Learn how markets work, and how they convey information
  • Understand Ricardo's theory of trade and specialization
  • Economics tells us that people with freedom contribute more to society
Learn more about Professor Coate

If a job interviewer asked you what economics has taught you about the world, what would you say? Here are Professor Coate's Responses!

1. I was not aware of the great increase in living standards over the last 200 years. This is called in economics, “the great enrichment.” Living standards may have gone up 100 times in Western Europe and America and at least 30 times in much of the rest of the world. The great enrichment resulted from the spread of capitalism and the displacement of mercantilism and feudalism. Ordinary people were no longer serfs or slaves but gained rights to property and contract and trade. The market price system emerged. This history provides strong evidence for capitalism or the market price system as the preferred system of social organization. Without it, as the past (and the present) demonstrate, people struggle to survive.

2. I had never heard before the argument for the market price system as a giant information processing system, revealing and coordinating the plans and actions of all of us as consumers and producers. Market prices result from the interaction of market participants on the demand and supply sides based on the knowledge and resources that each possess. They enable producers to discover which goods consumers demand and which production processes can most efficiently produce them. Capitalism succeeds because these free market prices adjust to reflect new information, allowing both consumers and producers to continuously update and coordinate their plans as all of us decide how best to lead our lives. One reason socialism fails everywhere and always is because government elites set prices when they don’t have the information to balance supply and demand across a large number of markets, many that are beyond their imagination. The price information they would need is available only one way, through a free market system where prices result from the interaction of market participants on the demand and supply side based on the knowledge and resources that each possess.

3. I was not aware of efficient markets theory and the difficulty of picking individual stocks for a portfolio. I thought I could study finance in college and learn how to be a “stock picker.” Alas, because stock prices at any time reflect all that is known about the prospects of a firm (due to the tireless research of market participants), it can be only a guess as to whether Apple is going to outperform Microsoft over the next six months. The stock price changes for these firms will be in response to information not yet known (what is known is already in the price). Although stock picking is nearly impossible, it is possible to be a savvy and wealthy stock market investor by buying index funds or ETFs that mirror the market. We don’t know in a particular year which stocks are going to go up or down, but we do know that the market as a whole will go up about 10% per year in nominal terms overtime. This has been the case since the Civil War. By the rule of 72, we know that this means your investment will double in value every 7 years as the miracle of compound interest work its magic.

Professor Coate's Given Definition of Economics

As Boettke reminds us - much of the following is copied from him - economics teaches us how social cooperation under the division of labor is realized. This is what determines whether nations are rich or poor; whether the individuals in these nations live in poverty, ignorance, and squalor, or live healthy and wealthy lives full of possibilities. If the institutions promote social cooperation under the division of labor, then the gains from trade and innovation will be realized. Individuals can specialize in what they do best and trade with others for what they do best. But if the institutions hinder social cooperation under the division of labor then life will devolve into a struggle for daily existence. Economics, in other words, gives us the key intellectual framework for understanding how we can live better together. We do this best through capitalism or the market price system. Individuals own themselves and the fruits of their labor, and have rights to property over what they purchase or create, and are free to trade with others, each then specializing in what they do best. The knowledge gained by each of us as we strive to improve our circumstances each day is revealed and organized and shared through trade and the market price system. Free individuals are not owned by their government, their community, or their neighbors. They are free to do as they wish as long as they don't trespass against others. This enables them to live a satisfying life because they can enhance the welfare of others through voluntary exchange while also taking the responsibility to reach their own potential. The moral basis of this system of social cooperation based on self ownership, private property, and freedom of contract is not that it produces great wealth, which it does, but that it gives liberty and peace. Individuals own the work of their hands and the thoughts of their brain. They are not serfs or slaves. To be procapitalist is to be antislavey. To be procapitalist is to support free contract and free association over forced exchange. To be procapitalist is to favor peace over violence.

Think in Supply Chains
Mauricio Caceres Bravo • Economics Assistant Professor • Baruch College Zicklin School of Business
“In an interdependent world, resilience matters. Learn negotiation and coalition-building.”
  • Trace how one product reaches you; list chokepoints.
  • Follow rules on digital trade and climate standards.
  • Practice interest-based negotiation in group projects.
